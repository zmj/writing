Falsehoods I believed about idempotency

July 12, 2026

For years I told people that making an endpoint idempotent was mostly a matter of adding a request ID and a unique constraint. This is the kind of thing that is true in a demo and false everywhere else.

The first crack appeared when a retry arrived while the original was still running. The unique constraint saved the data, but the client got a 500 and retried again. Nothing was corrupted, and yet everyone involved — the client, the on-call engineer, me — believed something had gone wrong. See also the Stripe docs on idempotency keys, which quietly assume more than they state.

The window nobody draws

Every diagram of a retry shows two neat, sequential arrows. The failure mode lives in the gap between them, and the gap is never drawn.

if seen(request_id):
    return stored_response(request_id)
# <- the entire problem lives here
process(request)

The check and the work are separated by real time. A second request can pass the check before the first records its result. You can close the window with a lock, or shrink it with an atomic insert-then-process, but you cannot wish it away — and most systems I have read simply wish it away.

What I believe now

Idempotency is not a property you add. It is a property you prove, operation by operation, against the specific ways your system fails. The request ID is bookkeeping; the proof is the work.

Once I started drawing the gap, the fixes became obvious — and much less pleasant than a unique constraint.
